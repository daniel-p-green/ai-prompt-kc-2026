# KC Streetcar Authority Dossier

Compiled: March 28, 2026

## Executive Summary

The Kansas City Streetcar Authority (KCSA) is the nonprofit entity responsible for managing, operating, and maintaining the KC Streetcar system and planning future extensions. Beyond operations, KCSA also leads system branding, marketing, public communication, and community engagement.

As of March 28, 2026, the KC Streetcar is a fare-free 5.7-mile streetcar line connecting River Market and UMKC. The original downtown route opened on May 6, 2016, and the Main Street Extension opened on October 24, 2025. KCSA works closely with the City of Kansas City, Missouri, the Kansas City Area Transportation Authority (KCATA), and operations contractor Herzog Transit.

The near-term system story is expansion. The Main Street Extension is already open, and the Riverfront Extension is positioned on the live site as "Opening Spring 2026," with construction listed at 99% complete in the latest posted update.

## System Snapshot

### Service Footprint

The streetcar system overview page says the KC Streetcar:

- runs from River Market to UMKC
- serves River Market, Downtown, Crossroads, Union Hill, Midtown, Westport, Country Club Plaza, and South Plaza
- has 31 platform stops according to the overview page
- spans 5.7 miles of track

### Service Model

- Fare-free to ride
- Peak frequency: approximately every 10 to 15 minutes
- Off-peak frequency: approximately every 12 to 18 minutes

### Hours of Operation

- Sunday through Thursday: 5:00 a.m. to midnight
- Friday: 5:00 a.m. to 1:00 a.m.
- Saturday: 5:00 a.m. to 1:00 a.m.

The site also says the streetcar runs on holidays with adjusted schedules.

### Travel Time

Per the FAQ:

- one-way travel time from UMKC to River Market is about 25 to 30 minutes
- a full loop is about 50 to 60 minutes, depending on traffic

## Fleet and Operations

The live FAQ states:

- fleet size: 14 streetcar vehicles as of 2025
- vehicle length: 77 feet 8 inches
- vehicle weight: 78,000 pounds
- vehicle height: 12 feet
- capacity: approximately 150 riders per vehicle
- vehicles are bi-directional
- each vehicle has four sets of doors

KCSA also states that:

- vehicles include interior and exterior cameras
- KCSA coordinates with KCPD and uses contract security
- riders must request stops by pressing the stop or door button unless passengers are waiting at a stop

The December 2025 board presentation surfaced on the site also indicates KCSA was expanding its security function into FY26, including the introduction of Tony Uredi in a security leadership role.

## Rider Experience

### Accessibility

The accessibility page states:

- all stops provide 100% level boarding
- middle doors are the recommended boarding point for wheelchairs, bikes, strollers, and other wheeled devices
- in a service outage, KCSA and KCATA provide a free auxiliary "bus bridge" along the route

### Basic Rider Flow

The how-to-ride page emphasizes:

- real-time arrival and service information at platforms
- same-level boarding at middle doors
- allowing riders to exit before boarding
- not holding doors open
- pressing the stop or door button to request a stop
- using crosswalks and looking before crossing after exiting

### Safety Framing

The safety page emphasizes:

- "Respect the Rail"
- cross tracks at a 90-degree angle when biking or using scooters
- use designated crosswalks
- stay alert around quiet rail vehicles
- observe the midtown transit-only lane rules now that the Main Street Extension is open

## Ridership and Demand Signals

The ridership page posts monthly charts, but several recent articles provide clearer text-based signals:

- In the first 10 days after the Main Street Extension opening, the system logged 116,899 passenger trips.
- The busiest day in that first 10-day window was November 1, 2025, with 16,816 trips.
- November 2025 produced 341,922 passenger trips, which the site describes as the highest monthly ridership in system history.
- As of that November 2025 update, year-to-date 2025 ridership was 1,799,708 trips.
- The same article says the KC Streetcar was accounting for approximately 30% of all transit trips in the Kansas City region.
- To handle higher demand, KCSA says operations expanded from a 3 to 4 streetcar daily rotation on the downtown line to 7 to 8 streetcars on the full system, with up to 9 streetcars during peak periods.

## Funding and Governance Structure

### KCSA vs. TDDs

One of the most important institutional distinctions is that KCSA is not the same entity as the transportation development districts that fund major portions of the system.

### Downtown Transportation Development District

The Downtown TDD page says the starter line is funded through:

- sales tax
- property assessments
- parking lot assessments

It also states that the Downtown TDD is separate from both KCSA and the City of Kansas City, Missouri.

### Main Street Rail TDD

The Main Street Rail TDD page says:

- voters approved local funding for the Main Street extension in spring 2018
- the TDD covers the area from the Missouri River south to 53rd Street, and from State Line Road east to Campbell Street
- the TDD can impose a 1% sales tax and special property assessments
- those revenues support operations, maintenance, and debt service tied to the streetcar system and Main Street extension

### FAQ Funding Detail

The FAQ adds that the downtown operating budget is comprised mostly of TDD-collected revenues, including:

- up to a 1% sales tax within the TDD boundary
- special assessments on real estate in the district
- a supplemental assessment on surface pay parking lots

## Expansion Outlook

### Main Street Extension

The Main Street Extension page says:

- passenger service opened October 24, 2025
- the opening followed federally required systems testing, safety validation, and operator training

### Riverfront Extension

The Riverfront Extension page says:

- opening target: Spring 2026
- January/February 2026 status: 99% complete
- the project will connect from 3rd Street and Grand Boulevard to Berkley Riverfront
- KCSA, KCATA, Port KC, and the City are delivery partners
- work on the Riverfront Streetcar Station and CPKC Pavilion is underway
- the pavilion is expected to be complete before the World Cup

The CPKC Pavilion page describes that future northern terminus as part of a 6.5-mile expanded system once the Riverfront Extension opens.

## Notable Inconsistencies and Follow-Up Items

These are the main items that should be verified before this dossier is used as polished external copy:

- Stop count conflict: the main overview page says `31 platform stops`, while the FAQ and sponsorship pages say `30 stops`.
- Staff count conflict: the KCSA page says day-to-day work is handled by a staff of six, but the meetings page lists a seventh named contact in Tony Uredi.
- Staff title conflict: several titles differ between the KCSA page and meetings page.
- Accessibility copy lag: the accessibility page still says the streetcar is free "from Union Station to the River Market," which appears to reflect pre-extension copy rather than the current River Market-to-UMKC line.
- Some current ridership figures are posted as images rather than text, which makes the public-facing page harder to cite precisely without companion news posts.

## Bottom Line

KCSA is now managing a materially larger, higher-demand transit system than the original downtown starter line. The institutional picture is straightforward at a high level: KCSA runs the streetcar, distinct TDDs fund key portions of it, and the city/KCATA/Herzog/Port KC ecosystem supplies delivery and operating support. The strategic story for 2026 is post-Main-Street ridership growth and a likely near-term Riverfront opening.
