## Top 5 reusable patterns for Kansas City

1. Own the coordination layer, not the food itself. The fastest programs to prove value in public settings were routing systems that sat on top of existing partners, not new brick-and-mortar supply. Philadelphia FAST is the clearest case, and 412 Food Rescue shows the logistics variant. ([PMC][1])

2. Make the resident front door dead simple: one place to look, one place to reserve, one place to get updates. Philadelphia Food Finder and NYC’s Plentiful both reduced uncertainty by centralizing hours, locations, and next steps, instead of forcing residents to decode scattered program lists. ([City of Philadelphia][3])

3. Time-slotting is underrated. Reservation systems do not create more food, but they do reduce opening-rush lines, failed trips, and staff chaos while generating usable operational data the city can renew against. ([Plentiful][12])

4. Use neighborhood hubs people already know. Libraries, schools, senior housing, community centers, church events, and transit-adjacent stops show up again and again because they lower discovery friction and travel burden. ([PMC][4])

5. If you move food physically, piggyback on existing networks. The more successful last-mile models used existing retailers, volunteers, or existing mobile units instead of asking the city to build and run a giant fleet on day one. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][5])

## Top 5 anti-patterns / traps

1. Starting with a new grocery store. Fare & Square and OKC both show the gap between a compelling vision and a 60-day proof: stores take years, subsidy, and hard retail execution. ([PMC][11])

2. Building a pretty finder with no data ownership. Philadelphia created Food Finder because scattered maps and blog posts became hard to maintain during a crisis. If KC cannot keep hours, closures, ID requirements, and language data current, the product will lose trust fast. ([City of Philadelphia][3])

3. Promising home delivery before solving payment and partner capacity. FAST relied on partner capacity; Baltimore’s online grocery models ran into SNAP/payment constraints historically and retailer tech issues more recently. Do not make citywide delivery the first promise. ([PMC][1])

4. Treating transportation as the whole solution. OKC’s shuttle was a sensible bridge, but the city still needed pop-ups and later a heavily financed store. Transportation can relieve pain, but by itself it is not a strong renewal story. ([Oklahoma City][10])

5. Doing corner-store or mobile retail without supply-chain math. DC’s Healthy Corners worked because it paired store recruitment with wholesale delivery, SNAP Match, and operating support. A corner-store pilot without procurement, refrigeration, and merchant buy-in is mostly theater. ([OCA][7])

The ideas that look attractive but are usually too slow, too expensive, or too policy-dependent for KC’s 60-day proof window are full new grocery builds or acquisitions, city-owned delivery fleets, broad corner-store conversions, and models that only work if retailer payment integrations or benefit-processing rules are fixed first. ([PMC][11])

## 3 pilot concepts Kansas City could plausibly execute in 60 days

1. **KC Food Access Router.** A resident-facing SMS/web tool that lets people search by ZIP or address, see pantry hours, language, ID rules, cold-storage availability, transit proximity, and reserve a pickup slot or opt into alerts. On the back end, pantries update capacity, closures, and service windows in a lightweight operator dashboard. This is the strongest blend of the Plentiful, Food Finder, and FAST patterns. ([nyc.gov][2])

2. **Transit-Adjacent Hub Scheduler.** A thin operations layer for 2–4 pickup hubs near strong bus service or recent closure zones, likely at libraries, churches, schools, or community centers. Residents book windows; operators push closure or overflow alerts; homebound overflow can be handled by a small volunteer pool. This borrows the strongest pieces of Baltimore VSP, OKC’s stopgap hub logic, and Arlington’s “food + referrals at the same place” pattern. ([PMC][4])

3. **Rescue Dispatch for Perishables.** A donor-to-pantry routing tool for surplus prepared food and produce, with volunteers or partner drivers claiming short-haul pickups and recipient pantries confirming capacity before dispatch. This takes the most measurable part of 412 Food Rescue and applies it to KC’s pantry strain and supply volatility. ([412 Food Rescue][6])

## 1 recommended concept with rationale

**Recommended concept: KC Food Access Router, with reservations and alerts first, and only a thin physical layer second.** In practice, that means a resident front door plus operator dashboard: search, reserve, alert, capacity, closures, and partner analytics. If you want a physical proof element, add 2–3 transit-adjacent pickup windows or pop-up hubs, not a new store and not a new fleet. This recommendation is the best fit because it copies the fastest, most reusable mechanics in the research set: a trusted front door from Philadelphia, reservation and client communication from NYC, and measurable coordination from FAST. It also avoids the slowest bets in the set: store building, heavy capex, and retailer-policy dependence. ([City of Philadelphia][3])

For a 60-day proof, I would judge success on operational indicators the city can actually renew against: partner listings kept current, reservations booked and completed, repeat users, alert engagement, failed-trip reduction, and partner capacity utilization. If KC wants a second metric family, add pounds rescued or overflow deliveries only after the routing layer shows where access is still breaking. That sequence is more realistic than trying to launch a store, a bus fleet, and a delivery program all at once. ([PMC][1])
